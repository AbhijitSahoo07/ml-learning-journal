# XLNet

## Overview
XLNet is a powerful natural language processing (NLP) model introduced by Google and Carnegie Mellon University in 2019. It stands out as a "generalized autoregressive pretraining method" that aims to combine the best of two worlds: the autoregressive (AR) models (like GPT) and autoencoding (AE) models (like BERT).

Traditional autoregressive models, such as those used for language generation, predict the next token based on previous tokens (e.g., left-to-right context). While excellent for generation, they struggle with tasks requiring a full understanding of the context from both directions. Autoencoding models like BERT, on the other hand, learn bidirectional context by masking certain tokens and predicting them based on the surrounding unmasked tokens. This gives BERT a strong understanding of context, making it highly effective for many NLP tasks.

However, BERT has its own limitations, primarily the "pretrain-finetune discrepancy" (the model sees `[MASK]` tokens during pretraining but not during finetuning) and the assumption that masked tokens are independent of each other. XLNet was designed to overcome these limitations by introducing a novel pretraining objective called **Permutation Language Modeling (PLM)**, while also incorporating ideas from Transformer-XL to handle long-range dependencies. In essence, XLNet learns deep bidirectional contexts without relying on masking, leading to more robust and powerful language representations.

## What Problem It Solves
XLNet primarily addresses the limitations inherent in previous dominant pretraining methods for NLP, specifically those of Autoregressive (AR) models and Autoencoding (AE) models like BERT.

1.  **Limitations of Autoregressive (AR) Models (e.g., GPT, Transformer-XL):**
    *   **Unidirectional Context:** AR models predict tokens sequentially, typically from left-to-right (e.g., $P(x_t | x_1, \dots, x_{t-1})$). This means they can only capture context from one direction. For many NLP tasks like question answering or sentiment analysis, understanding the full bidirectional context of a word is crucial. If a model only sees words to its left, it might miss important information from words to its right.
    *   **Suboptimal for Downstream Tasks:** Because of their unidirectional nature, AR models often don't learn as rich a representation for tasks that require a deep understanding of the entire sentence context.

2.  **Limitations of Autoencoding (AE) Models (e.g., BERT's Masked Language Model - MLM):**
    *   **Pretrain-Finetune Discrepancy:** During pretraining, BERT introduces special `[MASK]` tokens into the input sequence. The model then learns to predict the original tokens for these masked positions. However, during finetuning for downstream tasks, there are no `[MASK]` tokens in the input. This creates a mismatch between the pretraining and finetuning phases, potentially harming performance.
    *   **Independence Assumption:** BERT's MLM objective predicts masked tokens independently given the unmasked tokens. For example, if "New York" is masked, BERT predicts "New" and "York" separately, conditioned on the surrounding words but not on each other. This ignores the dependencies between the masked tokens themselves, which can be crucial for understanding phrases or multi-word expressions.
    *   **No Autoregressive Property:** While BERT learns bidirectional context, it doesn't have the autoregressive property, meaning it cannot be directly used for generative tasks like text generation without modifications.

XLNet was developed to overcome these challenges by proposing a pretraining objective that allows for learning bidirectional context without the pretrain-finetune discrepancy or the independence assumption of masked tokens, while also retaining the autoregressive nature for potential generative capabilities.

## How It Works
XLNet's core innovation lies in its **Permutation Language Modeling (PLM)** objective, combined with the architectural advancements of **Transformer-XL**. Let's break down its mechanism:

1.  **Permutation Language Modeling (PLM):**
    *   **The Core Idea:** Instead of predicting tokens in a fixed left-to-right order (like AR models) or masking tokens (like AE models), XLNet trains by predicting tokens in a *randomized permutation* of the input sequence.
    *   **How it works:** For a given input sequence $x = [x_1, x_2, \dots, x_T]$, XLNet considers all possible permutations of the token indices. For each permutation, it predicts each token $x_{z_t}$ based on the tokens that come *before* it in that specific permutation ($x_{z_{<t}}$).
    *   **Bidirectional Context without Masking:** By training on various permutations, a token $x_i$ can learn to predict $x_j$ where $x_j$ might originally appear to its left or right in the original sequence. This effectively allows the model to see context from both directions for any given token, similar to BERT, but without using `[MASK]` tokens. This eliminates the pretrain-finetune discrepancy.
    *   **Autoregressive Property:** Since it's still predicting tokens sequentially (just in a permuted order), it retains the autoregressive property, meaning it models the joint probability of the tokens, avoiding the independence assumption of BERT's MLM.

2.  **Two-Stream Self-Attention:**
    *   **The Challenge:** If we simply apply standard self-attention to a permuted sequence, when predicting $x_{z_t}$, the model would have access to the content of $x_{z_t}$ itself (because $x_{z_t}$ is part of the input sequence). This is information leakage and makes the prediction task trivial.
    *   **The Solution:** To prevent this, XLNet introduces a novel **Two-Stream Self-Attention** mechanism:
        *   **Content Stream (c-stream) Attention:** This stream is similar to standard Transformer self-attention. It computes representations $h_{z_t}$ that encode both the context $x_{z_{<t}}$ AND the content of $x_{z_t}$. This stream is used when computing representations for tokens that will serve as context for *future* predictions in the permutation.
        *   **Query Stream (q-stream) Attention:** This stream computes representations $g_{z_t}$ that encode only the context $x_{z_{<t}}$ AND the position of $x_{z_t}$, but NOT the content of $x_{z_t}$. This stream is specifically used to query and predict the token $x_{z_t}$. It knows *where* $x_{z_t}$ is, and what its preceding context is, but not *what* $x_{z_t}$ is.
    *   **How they interact:**
        *   For the first layer, the query stream is initialized with a learnable vector (e.g., `w`) and the content stream is initialized with the token embeddings.
        *   In subsequent layers, the query stream uses the previous layer's query stream output for its query, and the previous layer's content stream output for its key and value.
        *   The content stream uses the previous layer's content stream output for all its query, key, and value.
        *   This careful design ensures that when predicting $x_{z_t}$, the model only uses the query stream's representation $g_{z_t}$, which is prevented from seeing $x_{z_t}$'s content.

3.  **Integration with Transformer-XL:**
    *   XLNet builds upon the Transformer-XL architecture, which is designed to handle **long-range dependencies** more effectively than standard Transformers.
    *   **Segment Recurrence Mechanism:** Transformer-XL introduces a segment-level recurrence mechanism. Instead of processing a very long sequence as one chunk, it breaks it into segments. When processing the current segment, it reuses the hidden states from the previous segment, effectively extending the context window beyond the current segment's length. This allows information to flow across segments.
    *   **Relative Positional Encodings:** Instead of absolute positional encodings, Transformer-XL uses relative positional encodings. This means the model learns to attend based on the relative distance between tokens, which is more robust and generalizes better to longer sequences.
    *   By incorporating these features, XLNet can capture dependencies that span thousands of tokens, which is crucial for understanding complex documents.

**Training Process:**
1.  **Pretraining:** XLNet is pretrained on massive text corpora (like Wikipedia and BookCorpus) using the Permutation Language Modeling objective. The model learns to predict tokens in various permuted orders, leveraging the two-stream attention and Transformer-XL's recurrence.
2.  **Finetuning:** After pretraining, the XLNet model can be finetuned for specific downstream NLP tasks (e.g., question answering, sentiment analysis, text classification). For finetuning, typically only the content stream representations are used, and a task-specific classification head is added on top.

## Mathematical Intuition
Let's dive into the mathematical underpinnings of XLNet, focusing on the Permutation Language Modeling objective and the two-stream attention.

### 1. Autoregressive (AR) vs. Autoencoding (AE) Objectives

**Autoregressive (AR) Models (e.g., GPT):**
These models maximize the likelihood of a sequence $x = [x_1, \dots, x_T]$ by factorizing the probability in a fixed, typically left-to-right, order:
$$P(x) = \prod_{t=1}^T P(x_t | x_{<t})$$
where $x_{<t} = [x_1, \dots, x_{t-1}]$.
The objective is to maximize $\log P(x)$.

**Autoencoding (AE) Models (e.g., BERT's MLM):**
These models corrupt the input sequence (e.g., by masking tokens) and then learn to reconstruct the original tokens. For BERT, given a sequence $x$, a subset of tokens $\mathcal{M}$ is masked. The objective is to predict the masked tokens given the unmasked ones:
$$P(x_{\mathcal{M}} | x_{\text{unmasked}}) = \prod_{i \in \mathcal{M}} P(x_i | x_{\text{unmasked}})$$
The key issue here is the independence assumption: $P(x_i | x_{\text{unmasked}})$ assumes $x_i$ is independent of other masked tokens $x_j$ for $j \in \mathcal{M}, j \neq i$.

### 2. Permutation Language Modeling (PLM)

XLNet proposes a **generalized autoregressive objective** that combines the benefits of AR and AE models.
Given a sequence $x = [x_1, \dots, x_T]$, let $\mathcal{Z}_T$ be the set of all possible permutations of the indices $[1, \dots, T]$.
The PLM objective is to maximize the expected log-likelihood over all possible permutations:
$$E_{z \sim \mathcal{Z}_T} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^T \log P(x_{z_t} | x_{z_{<t}}) \right]$$
where $z = [z_1, \dots, z_T]$ is a permutation of $[1, \dots, T]$, and $x_{z_{<t}}$ denotes the tokens $x_{z_1}, \dots, x_{z_{t-1}}$ that precede $x_{z_t}$ in the permutation $z$.

**Intuition:**
*   By considering all permutations, for any target token $x_i$, its context $x_{z_{<t}}$ can include tokens from both its original left and right sides. This provides bidirectional context.
*   Since it's an autoregressive factorization, it models the joint probability $P(x_{z_t} | x_{z_{<t}})$, thus avoiding the independence assumption of BERT's MLM.
*   In practice, instead of sampling all $T!$ permutations, XLNet samples a subset of permutations and focuses on predicting a few target tokens at the end of each sampled permutation. For example, it might predict the last $K$ tokens in a permutation, given the preceding $T-K$ tokens.

### 3. Two-Stream Self-Attention

The PLM objective requires a special attention mechanism to prevent information leakage. When predicting $x_{z_t}$, the model should only see its position and preceding context, not its content. This is where the two-stream self-attention comes in.

Let $h_{z_t}^{(m)}$ be the content representation of token $x_{z_t}$ at layer $m$, and $g_{z_t}^{(m)}$ be the query representation of token $x_{z_t}$ at layer $m$.

**a) Content Stream Attention ($h$-stream):**
This stream is similar to standard Transformer attention. It computes a representation that encodes both the context and the content of the token itself.
$$h_{z_t}^{(m)} = \text{Attention}(Q=h_{z_t}^{(m-1)}, K=h_{z_{\le t}}^{(m-1)}, V=h_{z_{\le t}}^{(m-1)})$$
Here, $h_{z_{\le t}}^{(m-1)}$ means the content representations of all tokens up to and including $x_{z_t}$ in the current permutation. This stream has full access to the content of $x_{z_t}$. It's used to provide context for *future* predictions.
The initial content stream representation $h_{z_t}^{(0)}$ is the embedding of $x_{z_t}$.

**b) Query Stream Attention ($g$-stream):**
This stream computes a representation that encodes the context and the position of the token, but *not* its content. This is crucial for predicting $x_{z_t}$.
$$g_{z_t}^{(m)} = \text{Attention}(Q=g_{z_t}^{(m-1)}, K=h_{z_{<t}}^{(m-1)}, V=h_{z_{<t}}^{(m-1)})$$
Here, $h_{z_{<t}}^{(m-1)}$ means the content representations of all tokens *preceding* $x_{z_t}$ in the current permutation. Notice that $g_{z_t}^{(m)}$ does not attend to $h_{z_t}^{(m-1)}$ (the content of $x_{z_t}$ itself).
The initial query stream representation $g_{z_t}^{(0)}$ is a learnable vector (e.g., `w`) plus the positional encoding of $x_{z_t}$. It does not include the token embedding of $x_{z_t}$.

**Prediction:**
The final prediction for $x_{z_t}$ is made using the query stream representation $g_{z_t}^{(M)}$ from the last layer $M$:
$$P(x_{z_t} | x_{z_{<t}}) = \text{softmax}(W_k g_{z_t}^{(M)})$$
where $W_k$ is a weight matrix for the output layer.

This two-stream mechanism ensures that the model can learn bidirectional context through permutations while strictly preventing information leakage during the prediction of a target token.

### 4. Transformer-XL Integration

XLNet also incorporates the segment recurrence mechanism and relative positional encodings from Transformer-XL.
For a sequence split into segments $s_1, s_2, \dots, s_N$:
When processing segment $s_i$, the model reuses the hidden states from the previous segment $s_{i-1}$.
Let $H_{i-1}$ be the hidden states from segment $s_{i-1}$.
The attention computation for segment $s_i$ becomes:
$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{Q K^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V$$
where $K$ and $V$ are concatenated with the cached hidden states from the previous segment:
$$K = [H_{i-1}, H_i]$$
$$V = [H_{i-1}, H_i]$$
This allows the model to attend to a much longer context than just the current segment, effectively extending the receptive field and addressing long-range dependencies. Relative positional encodings further enhance this by making the model's understanding of position more robust to varying sequence lengths.

## Advantages
XLNet offers several significant advantages over previous models, particularly BERT:

*   **Addresses Pretrain-Finetune Discrepancy:** By using Permutation Language Modeling (PLM) instead of masking, XLNet eliminates the mismatch between pretraining (where `[MASK]` tokens are present) and finetuning (where they are not). This leads to more consistent and robust performance.
*   **Captures Bidirectional Context:** PLM allows XLNet to learn dependencies from both left and right contexts for any given token, similar to BERT, but without the limitations of masking. This results in a richer understanding of word meanings in context.
*   **Avoids Independence Assumption:** Unlike BERT's Masked Language Model, which assumes masked tokens are independent of each other, XLNet's autoregressive nature models the joint probability of tokens. This allows it to capture dependencies between predicted tokens, which is crucial for understanding phrases and multi-word expressions.
*   **Handles Long-Range Dependencies:** By integrating the segment recurrence mechanism and relative positional encodings from Transformer-XL, XLNet can effectively process and learn from very long sequences, capturing dependencies that span thousands of tokens. This is a major advantage for document-level tasks.
*   **Stronger Performance:** Due to these architectural and objective improvements, XLNet often achieves state-of-the-art results on a wide range of NLP tasks, outperforming BERT on many benchmarks, especially those requiring deeper contextual understanding or long-range reasoning (e.g., question answering, natural language inference).
*   **Generalized Autoregressive Model:** While primarily designed for understanding, its autoregressive nature means it can potentially be adapted for generative tasks more naturally than BERT, although it's not its primary use case.

## Disadvantages
Despite its powerful capabilities, XLNet also comes with certain drawbacks:

*   **Higher Computational Cost:**
    *   **Permutation Generation:** Generating and processing different permutations of the input sequence during training is computationally more expensive than BERT's fixed masking strategy.
    *   **Two-Stream Attention:** Maintaining two separate streams of attention (content and query) and their interactions adds significant computational overhead compared to a single-stream attention mechanism.
    *   **Transformer-XL Recurrence:** While beneficial for long contexts, managing and reusing hidden states across segments adds complexity and memory requirements.
*   **Increased Training Time:** The higher computational cost directly translates to longer training times, requiring more powerful hardware and resources.
*   **Complexity:** The architecture and training objective are more intricate than BERT's, making it harder to understand, implement from scratch, and debug.
*   **Memory Footprint:** The two-stream attention and segment recurrence can lead to a larger memory footprint, especially for longer sequences or larger batch sizes.
*   **Sampling Bias in Permutations:** While theoretically considering all permutations, in practice, only a subset is sampled. This might introduce a slight bias, though its impact is generally considered minor given the vast number of possible permutations.
*   **Not as Widely Adopted for Generation:** While it has an autoregressive component, XLNet is primarily optimized for understanding and classification tasks. Other models like GPT are still more commonly used for pure text generation due to their simpler, direct autoregressive formulation.

## Real World Applications
XLNet's ability to capture deep bidirectional context and handle long-range dependencies makes it highly effective across various real-world NLP applications:

1.  **Question Answering (QA) Systems:** XLNet excels in tasks like SQuAD (Stanford Question Answering Dataset) where the model needs to read a passage and extract the answer to a given question. Its deep contextual understanding and ability to process long documents (thanks to Transformer-XL) allow it to pinpoint precise answers even when they are subtly implied or located far from the question's keywords.
2.  **Text Summarization:** For both extractive and abstractive summarization, XLNet can generate more coherent and contextually relevant summaries. Its understanding of long-range dependencies helps it identify key information and relationships across an entire document, leading to better compression and retention of important details.
3.  **Sentiment Analysis and Opinion Mining:** XLNet can accurately classify the sentiment (positive, negative, neutral) of reviews, social media posts, or customer feedback. Its ability to capture nuanced contextual cues, including negations and complex sentence structures, allows for more precise sentiment detection, which is valuable for brand monitoring, customer service, and market research.
4.  **Named Entity Recognition (NER):** Identifying and classifying named entities (like persons, organizations, locations, dates) within text is crucial for information extraction. XLNet's robust contextual representations help it distinguish between different types of entities and resolve ambiguities, improving the accuracy of NER systems used in legal tech, healthcare, and financial services.
5.  **Information Retrieval and Search Engines:** By generating high-quality embeddings for queries and documents, XLNet can power more intelligent search engines. It can better understand the intent behind a user's query and retrieve more relevant documents, even if they don't contain exact keyword matches, by grasping semantic similarity and contextual relevance.

## Python Example
This example demonstrates how to use a pre-trained XLNet model from the Hugging Face `transformers` library for sequence classification. We'll use a dummy dataset for simplicity.

```python
import torch
from transformers import XLNetTokenizer, XLNetForSequenceClassification
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader, TensorDataset
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score, precision_recall_fscore_support
import numpy as np

# 1. Load pre-trained XLNet tokenizer and model
# 'xlnet-base-cased' is a common choice for general NLP tasks.
# We specify num_labels=2 for a binary classification task.
tokenizer = XLNetTokenizer.from_pretrained('xlnet-base-cased')
model = XLNetForSequenceClassification.from_pretrained('xlnet-base-cased', num_labels=2)

# Set the model to evaluation mode (important for inference)
model.eval()

print("XLNet tokenizer and model loaded successfully.")

# 2. Create a dummy dataset
# Let's simulate a simple sentiment analysis task (positive/negative)
texts = [
    "This movie was absolutely fantastic and I loved every minute of it!",
    "The food was terrible, I would never recommend this restaurant.",
    "It was an okay experience, nothing special.",
    "I had a great time, the staff was very friendly and helpful.",
    "What a waste of money, completely disappointed.",
    "An excellent book, highly engaging and thought-provoking.",
    "Mediocre at best, I expected more.",
    "Simply brilliant! A must-watch for everyone.",
    "Worst service ever, avoid at all costs.",
    "Pretty good, I might watch it again."
]
# Dummy labels: 0 for negative/neutral, 1 for positive
labels = [1, 0, 0, 1, 0, 1, 0, 1, 0, 1]

print(f"\nDummy dataset created with {len(texts)} samples.")

# 3. Tokenize and prepare the dataset
# We need to convert text into numerical input IDs, attention masks, and token type IDs.
# max_length: Pad/truncate sequences to a fixed length.
# padding='max_length': Pad to max_length.
# truncation=True: Truncate sequences longer than max_length.
# return_tensors='pt': Return PyTorch tensors.
input_ids = []
attention_masks = []
token_type_ids = [] # XLNet uses token_type_ids for segment separation (e.g., for QA)

for text in texts:
    encoded_dict = tokenizer.encode_plus(
                        text,                      # Sentence to encode.
                        add_special_tokens = True, # Add '[CLS]' and '[SEP]'
                        max_length = 64,           # Pad & truncate all sentences.
                        padding = 'max_length',
                        return_attention_mask = True,   # Construct attn. masks.
                        return_token_type_ids = True,   # Return token type IDs.
                        return_tensors = 'pt',     # Return pytorch tensors.
                   )
    
    input_ids.append(encoded_dict['input_ids'])
    attention_masks.append(encoded_dict['attention_mask'])
    token_type_ids.append(encoded_dict['token_type_ids'])

# Concatenate the lists of tensors into single tensors
input_ids = torch.cat(input_ids, dim=0)
attention_masks = torch.cat(attention_masks, dim=0)
token_type_ids = torch.cat(token_type_ids, dim=0)
labels = torch.tensor(labels)

print(f"Tokenized input_ids shape: {input_ids.shape}")
print(f"Tokenized attention_masks shape: {attention_masks.shape}")
print(f"Tokenized token_type_ids shape: {token_type_ids.shape}")
print(f"Labels shape: {labels.shape}")

# 4. Split data into training and validation sets
train_inputs, val_inputs, train_labels, val_labels = train_test_split(input_ids, labels, random_state=42, test_size=0.2)
train_masks, val_masks, _, _ = train_test_split(attention_masks, labels, random_state=42, test_size=0.2)
train_token_types, val_token_types, _, _ = train_test_split(token_type_ids, labels, random_state=42, test_size=0.2)

# Create TensorDatasets
train_dataset = TensorDataset(train_inputs, train_masks, train_token_types, train_labels)
val_dataset = TensorDataset(val_inputs, val_masks, val_token_types, val_labels)

# Create DataLoaders
batch_size = 2 # Small batch size for dummy data
train_dataloader = DataLoader(train_dataset, batch_size=batch_size, shuffle=True)
val_dataloader = DataLoader(val_dataset, batch_size=batch_size, shuffle=False)

print(f"\nDataset split into {len(train_dataset)} training and {len(val_dataset)} validation samples.")

# 5. Finetune the model (simplified training loop)
# In a real scenario, you'd train for multiple epochs with an optimizer and learning rate scheduler.
# For this example, we'll do a very basic training loop for one epoch.

# Set model to training mode
model.train()

# Define a simple optimizer
optimizer = torch.optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=2e-5)

print("\nStarting simplified training loop...")
for epoch in range(1): # Train for 1 epoch
    total_loss = 0
    for batch in train_dataloader:
        b_input_ids, b_input_mask, b_token_type_ids, b_labels = batch
        
        # Clear previous gradients
        optimizer.zero_grad()
        
        # Forward pass
        # XLNetForSequenceClassification expects input_ids, attention_mask, token_type_ids, and labels
        outputs = model(input_ids=b_input_ids, 
                        attention_mask=b_input_mask, 
                        token_type_ids=b_token_type_ids, 
                        labels=b_labels)
        
        loss = outputs.loss
        logits = outputs.logits
        total_loss += loss.item()
        
        # Backward pass and optimize
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()
    
    avg_train_loss = total_loss / len(train_dataloader)
    print(f"  Epoch 1 - Average training loss: {avg_train_loss:.4f}")

print("Training complete.")

# 6. Evaluate the model on the validation set
model.eval() # Set model to evaluation mode

predictions = []
true_labels = []

print("\nEvaluating on validation set...")
for batch in val_dataloader:
    b_input_ids, b_input_mask, b_token_type_ids, b_labels = batch
    
    with torch.no_grad(): # Disable gradient calculation for evaluation
        outputs = model(input_ids=b_input_ids, 
                        attention_mask=b_input_mask, 
                        token_type_ids=b_token_type_ids)
    
    logits = outputs.logits
    
    # Move logits and labels to CPU for numpy operations
    logits = logits.detach().cpu().numpy()
    label_ids = b_labels.to('cpu').numpy()
    
    # Store predictions and true labels
    predictions.extend(np.argmax(logits, axis=1).flatten())
    true_labels.extend(label_ids.flatten())

# Calculate metrics
accuracy = accuracy_score(true_labels, predictions)
precision, recall, f1, _ = precision_recall_fscore_support(true_labels, predictions, average='binary')

print(f"  Validation Accuracy: {accuracy:.4f}")
print(f"  Validation Precision: {precision:.4f}")
print(f"  Validation Recall: {recall:.4f}")
print(f"  Validation F1-Score: {f1:.4f}")

# 7. Make a prediction on a new, unseen sentence
new_sentence = "This product is absolutely amazing, I'm so happy with my purchase!"

# Tokenize the new sentence
encoded_new_sentence = tokenizer.encode_plus(
                            new_sentence,
                            add_special_tokens = True,
                            max_length = 64,
                            padding = 'max_length',
                            return_attention_mask = True,
                            return_token_type_ids = True,
                            return_tensors = 'pt',
                        )

new_input_ids = encoded_new_sentence['input_ids']
new_attention_mask = encoded_new_sentence['attention_mask']
new_token_type_ids = encoded_new_sentence['token_type_ids']

# Make prediction
model.eval() # Ensure model is in evaluation mode
with torch.no_grad():
    outputs = model(input_ids=new_input_ids, 
                    attention_mask=new_attention_mask, 
                    token_type_ids=new_token_type_ids)

logits = outputs.logits
predicted_class_id = torch.argmax(logits, dim=1).item()

sentiment_map = {0: "Negative/Neutral", 1: "Positive"}
print(f"\nPrediction for '{new_sentence}': {sentiment_map[predicted_class_id]}")

# Example of getting raw embeddings (if you wanted to use them for other tasks)
# To get embeddings, you'd typically use XLNetModel and extract the last hidden state.
# For XLNetForSequenceClassification, the last hidden state is passed to the classification head.
# Let's show how to get the last hidden state from XLNetModel.
from transformers import XLNetModel
xlnet_base_model = XLNetModel.from_pretrained('xlnet-base-cased')
xlnet_base_model.eval()

with torch.no_grad():
    outputs = xlnet_base_model(input_ids=new_input_ids, 
                               attention_mask=new_attention_mask, 
                               token_type_ids=new_token_type_ids)
    last_hidden_state = outputs.last_hidden_state # Shape: (batch_size, sequence_length, hidden_size)

# Often, the representation of the special '[CLS]' token (or the last token in XLNet)
# or an average of all tokens is used as the sentence embedding.
# For XLNet, the last token (which is '[CLS]' in the XLNet tokenization scheme)
# is often used for classification.
sentence_embedding = last_hidden_state[:, -1, :] # Get the embedding of the last token (CLS)
print(f"\nSentence embedding shape (from XLNetModel): {sentence_embedding.shape}")
print(f"First 5 dimensions of sentence embedding:\n{sentence_embedding[:, :5].numpy()}")
```

**Explanation of the Code:**

1.  **Load Model and Tokenizer:** We use `XLNetTokenizer` to convert text into numerical IDs and `XLNetForSequenceClassification` which is a pre-trained XLNet model with a classification head on top, suitable for tasks like sentiment analysis.
2.  **Dummy Dataset:** A small list of sentences and corresponding binary labels (0 or 1) is created to simulate a classification task.
3.  **Tokenization:** Each sentence is tokenized using `tokenizer.encode_plus`. This function handles:
    *   Converting words to subword tokens.
    *   Adding special tokens (`<cls>` and `<sep>`) that XLNet expects.
    *   Padding shorter sequences and truncating longer ones to a `max_length`.
    *   Creating `attention_mask` (to distinguish real tokens from padding tokens) and `token_type_ids` (to distinguish different segments in tasks like QA, though here it's mostly uniform for single sentences).
    *   Returning PyTorch tensors.
4.  **Data Preparation:** The tokenized inputs and labels are converted into `TensorDataset` and `DataLoader` objects, which are standard PyTorch utilities for managing data in batches during training.
5.  **Finetuning Loop (Simplified):**
    *   The model is set to `train()` mode.
    *   An `AdamW` optimizer is used.

## Interview Questions

Here are at least 10 relevant technical interview questions about XLNet, complete with comprehensive, detailed answers:

1.  **What is XLNet, and what core problem does it aim to solve?**
    *   **Answer:** XLNet is a generalized autoregressive pretraining method for natural language understanding. It aims to combine the strengths of autoregressive (AR) models (like GPT, which model joint probability and avoid independence assumptions) and autoencoding (AE) models (like BERT, which learn bidirectional context). The core problems it solves are:
        1.  **Pretrain-Finetune Discrepancy in BERT:** BERT uses `[MASK]` tokens during pretraining but not during finetuning, creating a mismatch.
        2.  **Independence Assumption in BERT's MLM:** BERT assumes masked tokens are predicted independently, ignoring dependencies between them.
        3.  **Unidirectional Context in AR Models:** Traditional AR models only learn context from one direction (e.g., left-to-right), which is suboptimal for many NLU tasks.

2.  **Explain Permutation Language Modeling (PLM). How does it enable bidirectional context without masking?**
    *   **Answer:** PLM is XLNet's novel pretraining objective. Instead of predicting tokens in a fixed left-to-right order or masking tokens, PLM trains the model to predict tokens in a *randomized permutation* of the input sequence. For a given sequence $x$, it samples a permutation $z$ of its indices and then maximizes the log-likelihood of predicting each token $x_{z_t}$ based on the tokens that precede it in *that specific permutation* ($x_{z_{<t}}$).
    *   This enables bidirectional context because, across different permutations, any token $x_i$ can appear before or after any other token $x_j$. Thus, $x_i$ learns to predict $x_j$ using context from both its original left and right sides. It avoids masking, thus resolving the pretrain-finetune discrepancy.

3.  **Describe the Two-Stream Self-Attention mechanism in XLNet. Why is it necessary?**
    *   **Answer:** The two-stream self-attention is crucial for PLM to work without information leakage. When predicting a token $x_{z_t}$ in a permuted sequence, the model should not have access to the content of $x_{z_t}$ itself.
    *   It consists of:
        1.  **Content Stream (h-stream):** This stream computes representations $h_{z_t}$ that encode both the context $x_{z_{<t}}$ AND the content of $x_{z_t}$. It's similar to standard Transformer attention and is used when a token's representation needs to serve as context for *future* predictions.
        2.  **Query Stream (g-stream):** This stream computes representations $g_{z_t}$ that encode only the context $x_{z_{<t}}$ AND the position of $x_{z_t}$, but NOT the content of $x_{z_t}$. This stream is specifically used to query and predict the target token $x_{z_t}$.
    *   It's necessary because if only one stream were used, when predicting $x_{z_t}$, the model would trivially "see" the content of $x_{z_t}$ itself, making the prediction task meaningless. The query stream's restricted attention ensures the prediction is based purely on context and position.

4.  **How does XLNet handle long-range dependencies?**
    *   **Answer:** XLNet leverages the **Transformer-XL** architecture to handle long-range dependencies. Transformer-XL introduces two key mechanisms:
        1.  **Segment Recurrence Mechanism:** It processes long sequences by breaking them into segments. When processing the current segment, it reuses (caches) the hidden states from previous segments. This allows information to flow across segment boundaries, effectively extending the context window beyond the current segment's length.
        2.  **Relative Positional Encodings:** Instead of absolute positional encodings, Transformer-XL uses relative positional encodings. This means the model learns to attend based on the relative distance between tokens, which is more robust and generalizes better to longer sequences without needing to know the absolute position of a token within the entire document.

5.  **Compare and contrast XLNet with BERT. What are the key differences in their pretraining objectives and architectural choices?**
    *   **Answer:**
        *   **Pretraining Objective:**
            *   **BERT:** Uses Masked Language Modeling (MLM), where a percentage of tokens are masked and predicted independently based on unmasked context.
            *   **XLNet:** Uses Permutation Language Modeling (PLM), where tokens are predicted autoregressively based on a permuted sequence, allowing bidirectional context without masking.
        *   **Information Flow:**
            *   **BERT:** Learns bidirectional context by seeing tokens on both sides of a masked word. However, it suffers from the pretrain-finetune discrepancy and the independence assumption of masked tokens.
            *   **XLNet:** Learns bidirectional context through permutations, avoiding the pretrain-finetune discrepancy and modeling joint probabilities (no independence assumption).
        *   **Architecture:**
            *   **BERT:** Standard Transformer encoder.
            *   **XLNet:** Based on Transformer-XL, incorporating segment recurrence and relative positional encodings for better handling of long-range dependencies. It also uses the two-stream self-attention.
        *   **Generative Capability:**
            *   **BERT:** Not inherently generative due to masking; requires modifications for generation.
            *   **XLNet:** Inherently autoregressive (though in a permuted sense), making it more amenable to generative tasks, though not its primary focus.

6.  **What is the "pretrain-finetune discrepancy," and how does XLNet specifically address it?**
    *   **Answer:** The pretrain-finetune discrepancy refers to the difference in input distribution between the pretraining and finetuning phases of models like BERT. During pretraining, BERT introduces special `[MASK]` tokens into the input. However, during finetuning for downstream tasks, there are no `[MASK]` tokens. This means the model's input representation during finetuning is different from what it learned during pretraining, potentially leading to suboptimal performance.
    *   XLNet addresses this by using Permutation Language Modeling (PLM). Since PLM predicts actual tokens in a permuted order, it never introduces artificial `[MASK]` tokens into the input. The input sequences during pretraining are therefore identical in nature to the input sequences during finetuning, eliminating this discrepancy.

7.  **What are the main computational challenges or disadvantages of using XLNet compared to BERT?**
    *   **Answer:** XLNet is generally more computationally expensive than BERT.
        1.  **Permutation Overhead:** Generating and processing multiple permutations of the input sequence adds significant computational cost during pretraining.
        2.  **Two-Stream Attention:** Maintaining two separate attention streams (content and query) and their interactions doubles the attention computations per layer compared to a single-stream model.
        3.  **Transformer-XL Recurrence:** While beneficial, managing and reusing hidden states from previous segments adds complexity and memory overhead.
        4.  **Training Time:** These factors lead to longer training times and require more powerful hardware resources.

8.  **Can XLNet be used for text generation? Why or why not?**
    *   **Answer:** Yes, XLNet can be used for text generation, although it's not its primary strength or most common application compared to models like GPT. The reason it *can* be used is its autoregressive nature. Since the PLM objective trains the model to predict tokens sequentially (albeit in a permuted order), it learns to model the conditional probability of tokens given their predecessors. This fundamental property is what enables text generation. However, because its pretraining involves permutations, it's not as straightforward for left-to-right generation as a purely left-to-right AR model like GPT.

9.  **Explain the role of the `token_type_ids` in XLNet (and other Transformer models like BERT).**
    *   **Answer:** `token_type_ids` (also known as segment IDs) are used to distinguish between different segments in the input sequence, especially for tasks that involve two sentences, like Question Answering (QA) or Natural Language Inference (NLI). For example, in QA, the input might be `[CLS] question [SEP] passage [SEP]`. `token_type_ids` would assign one ID (e.g., 0) to the question tokens and another ID (e.g., 1) to the passage tokens. This helps the model understand which token belongs to which segment, allowing it to learn relationships *between* segments. For single-sentence tasks, they are often all set to 0.

10. **In what scenarios would you prefer XLNet over BERT, and vice versa?**
    *   **Answer:**
        *   **Prefer XLNet when:**
            *   **Tasks require deep contextual understanding and long-range dependencies:** XLNet's PLM and Transformer-XL integration make it superior for tasks like complex Question Answering, document summarization, or natural language inference where understanding relationships across long texts is critical.
            *   **Avoiding pretrain-finetune discrepancy is paramount:** If the downstream task is sensitive to the `[MASK]` token artifact, XLNet offers a cleaner solution.
            *   **Modeling joint probabilities of tokens is important:** For tasks where dependencies between predicted tokens are crucial, XLNet's autoregressive nature is beneficial.
        *   **Prefer BERT when:**
            *   **Computational resources are limited:** BERT is generally less computationally intensive to train and fine-tune.
            *   **Simplicity and speed are priorities:** BERT's architecture and objective are simpler, making it faster for many common tasks, especially when long-range dependencies are not a major factor.
            *   **The task doesn't heavily rely on the specific issues XLNet addresses:** For many standard classification or token-level tasks on shorter texts, BERT often performs very well with less overhead.

### Answer Key
